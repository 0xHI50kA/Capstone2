Tuberculosis (TB)

Tuberculosis (TB) is a contagious bacterial infection caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis. It primarily affects the lungs but can also spread to other parts of the body, including the kidneys, spine, and brain. If left untreated, TB can be life-threatening.

What is Tuberculosis?

TB is an airborne disease that spreads when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or speaks, releasing bacteria into the air. While some people may carry TB bacteria without symptoms (latent TB), others develop active TB, which requires immediate treatment.

How Does It Happen?

When TB bacteria enter the body, they can remain dormant for years without causing illness. However, if the immune system weakens, the bacteria become active, leading to TB symptoms and making the disease contagious.

Symptoms of Tuberculosis

  • Persistent cough lasting more than three weeks
  • Coughing up blood or mucus
  • Chest pain and difficulty breathing
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Fever and chills
  • Night sweats
  • Fatigue and weakness

Severe Tuberculosis

Without proper treatment, TB can spread to other organs, leading to severe complications such as:

  • Lung damage and respiratory failure
  • TB meningitis (affecting the brain and spinal cord)
  • Kidney and liver damage
  • Bone and joint infections
  • Multi-drug resistant TB (MDR-TB), which is harder to treat

How to Treat Tuberculosis

TB is treatable with a combination of antibiotics. Treatment typically includes:

  • A six-month course of multiple antibiotics (e.g., isoniazid, rifampin, ethambutol, pyrazinamide)
  • Directly observed therapy (DOT) to ensure proper medication adherence
  • Additional treatment for drug-resistant TB cases
  • Regular medical check-ups to monitor progress

Prevention of Tuberculosis

Preventing TB involves reducing exposure to infected individuals and strengthening the immune system. Preventive measures include:

  • Vaccination with the Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) vaccine
  • Early detection and treatment of active TB cases
  • Wearing masks and practicing proper respiratory hygiene
  • Maintaining good ventilation in indoor spaces
  • Regular TB screening for high-risk individuals

When to See a Doctor

Seek medical attention if you experience:

  • Persistent cough lasting more than three weeks
  • Coughing up blood
  • Unexplained weight loss and fatigue
  • Fever, chills, and night sweats
  • Exposure to someone diagnosed with active TB

Note: Tuberculosis is a serious disease, but early diagnosis and proper treatment can lead to a full recovery. If you experience symptoms or have been exposed to TB, seek medical advice immediately.
